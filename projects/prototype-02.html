Project Case Study

CLEVER

EVA Sample Size Location Calibration Marker for Lunar Surface EVA Operations

Timeline

October 2021 - June 2022

Project Type

Minimum Viable Product

Direct Affiliation

NASA Micro-g NExT 2021-22 Challenge (Neutral Buoyancy Laboratory - NBL)

Team Size

7 members Isaac Gonzalez Shafial Zaman Michael De la Pena Erik Arias-Gonzales Samantha Stukey-Reynoso Rain Ramos Bernardo Portillo

Affiliations

Sponsors

Context

NASA’s EC7 team supports the development of Extravehicular Activity (EVA) tools and equipment evaluated at the NBL in Houston. As NASA prepares for the Artemis lunar surface operations, the team continues to advance tool concepts related to sample collection, handling, and exploration EVA tasks. For this Micro-g NExT challenge, NASA presented 3 device options for collegiate teams to address through technical design proposals. After review and selection by NASA, accepted teams advanced into a multi-month process of designing, building, and refining their own prototype solutions in preparation for a week-long test campaign conducted in a simulated microgravity environment at the NBL. As for a CS major like myself, I ended up deciding to join this challenge since it would benefit as an introduction to the engineering design process, despite NExT primarily being targeted for MechE or Material Science majors. As part of the Design Subteam on Team Falcons representing Cerritos College, we choose the EVA Sample Size Location Calibration Marker as our device choice for the proposal.

Objective

Design a sample marker that can be deployed by crew members on the lunar surface. The design should focus on ease of use with limited hand dexterity and body positioning in the spacesuit. The purpose of the sample marker is to provide real-time data about a sample or rock to the scientists back on Earth. Using the marker, scientists will be able to identify information such as location, size and color of the sample, which will be helpful in identifying the type of rock. While the scientists decide if that sample should be collected, the astronauts can place markers at other sampling locations. The sample markers can be picked back up after sample collection and used in other sampling locations.

Overview

Sometime after the submission of the team’s proposal, the team was honored to be selected among the Top 15 teams across the nation to move onto the next phase as the only community college team, tasked with creating CLEVER, a system inspired by thumb push puppets that stand and collapse based on tension. It is designed to be deployed and stowed easily and efficiently with limited movement and dexterity in heavy gloves as the astronaut only needs to interact with the top section. When actuated with a handle push, CLEVER extends three tripod legs as a base to increase the height of the marker. It locks into tripod form until the astronaut releases the lock with a simple push and twist. While deployed, it stands tall enough to eliminate the need for the astronaut to bend down to retrieve it due to their limited movements in their suit. The alpha-numeric tag is double sided and made clearly visible to spot from either side, along with the color panel containing the six required color blocks for photometric calibration due to the moon’s atmosphere. Inner springs inside the body cause CLEVER to articulate itself back into its stowed configuration when unlocked. Its stowed size and weight was designed for easy fitting within the required 2” x 4” x 15” volume and 1 lb weight that an astronaut would require in order to carry it with them during a long lunar EVA mission.

During the project's timeline, I had the privilege to be advised by Professor Janet McLarty (Physics) and Professor Allen Bakalyar (Engineering).
