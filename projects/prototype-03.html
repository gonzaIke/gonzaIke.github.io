Context

NASA’s EC7 team supports the development of Extravehicular Activity (EVA) tools and equipment evaluated at the NBL in Houston. As NASA prepares for the Artemis lunar surface operations, the team continues to advance tool concepts related to sample collection, handling, and exploration EVA tasks. For this Micro-g NExT challenge, NASA presented 2 device options for collegiate teams to address through technical design proposals. I decided to start up Team Space Bears at Berkeley, where I took on the role as Team Lead to design a solution to one of the device options under the anchoring category, known as the Adjustable Tool Cart Handle (ATCH), for our device choice in the team’s proposal.

Objective

Design an adjustable tool cart handle (ATCH) that allows 1st – 99th percentile astronauts to easily traverse the lunar surface. The ATCH should allow astronauts to push, maneuver, and quickly stop. The handle adjustment mechanism must be glove-compatible to allow reliable, safe EVA actuation. The handle adjustment mechanism must also be dust-tolerant, as residual lunar regolith on the gloves is sure to make its way onto the tool cart handle. Astronauts will use the device with limited mobility in a space suit, so carefully consider the ergonomics of your device’s operations.

Overview

The team designed the Berkeley Adjustable Tool Cart Handle (BATCH), a glove friendly adjustment system for a tool cart handle. BATCH employs a pin insertion system inspired by scalp massagers and adjustable workout benches, that utilizes a low-effort pull of a pin from the astronaut to adjust the handlebar’s height. When free to move, the handlebar will be able to rotate in an arc. BATCH is designed for ease of use in thick gloves taking into consideration limited range of motion and reduced dexterity. The handle and the knob attached to the moving pin have more organic shapes and heavy texturing, increasing the purchase of gloves and reducing needed grip pressure. Its locked size and weight was designed for easy fitting within the required 16” x 16” x 20” volume and 5 lb weight. BATCH is simple to use and doesn’t impede on astronaut operations, making the complexity of lunar operations easier.

During the project's timeline, I had the privilege to be advised by Dr. Eric Paulos (Electrical Engineering & Computer Sciences).
