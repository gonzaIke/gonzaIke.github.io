Project Case Study

URSA

Intuitive AR UI using non-intrusive display and LLM-based voice control

Context

NASA’s Joint AR team supports the development of augmented reality systems and visual informatics tools for human spaceflight operations at Johnson Space Center. As NASA prepares for the Artemis lunar surface missions, the team continues to advance suit-integrated display concepts that can improve astronaut navigation, task execution, communication, and EVA situational awareness on both the Moon and Mars. For this NASA SUITS challenge, collegiate teams explored how augmented reality and localized mission control interfaces can support astronauts during future Mars surface operations, which must be addressed through technical design proposals. During EVAs, astronauts must navigate terrain, complete science and exploration tasks, monitor spacesuit data, and coordinate with nearby mission support systems. Because Mars missions may experience communication delays of up to 20 minutes each way, crews will need more autonomous, real-time access to mission information through non-obtrusive AR displays (HMD) and local control consoles (LMCC). After co-founding a NASA-based RSO at Berkeley, Team SPACE BEARS was put together in collaboration between UC Berkeley and Baylor University to complete the proposal, and after successfully being selected as one of the Top 11 teams in the nation, we then partnered up with the FHL Vive Lab on Berkeley campus to accelerate the project’s growth for a successful test week.

Objective

Design and develop an HMD-based augmented reality interface to support a Mars EVA scenario at NASA Johnson Space Center’s Rock Yard. The HMD interface needed to assist an EVA crew member during field operations with a rover while accounting for outdoor lighting and usability constraints. Its systems were required to communicate through NASA’s Telemetry Stream Server (TSS) and support coordinated mission operations between the EVA crewmember and the local mission control.

Overview

URSA is an LLM-driven immersive AR interface designed to explore how astronauts can interact with robots and mission data in high-pressure space environments without adding unnecessary visual, physical, or cognitive burden. The project centers on a non-intrusive head-mounted display experience, using the HoloLens 2 to keep the astronaut’s real-world view unobstructed while placing essential information in peripheral or minimally invasive areas. Rather than relying on hand gestures or gaze controls, the interface prioritizes voice control so astronauts can operate AR panels, maps, and rover related tools while keeping their hands and attention available for mission-critical tasks. This design choice responds directly to the constraints of space suits, where limited mobility and constant environmental awareness make traditional AR interaction patterns less practical.

A virtual voice assistant, Ursa, translates spoken commands into interface actions through an LLM-based function-calling pipeline, allowing users to request actions like opening maps or changing interface views through natural language. The seamless value of the system comes from reducing interaction friction: astronauts do not need to navigate complex menus, obscure their field of view, or perform precise gestures in a constrained environment. The interface also integrates real-time mission information, including suit telemetry, EVA task status, navigation maps, and rover location overlays, while preserving situational awareness.

Beyond the interface layer, the project connects AR interaction with robotics perception by tracking the Leo Rover in real time and displaying its position as an AR overlay. To support this, the team built a robot-specific digital twin tracking dataset using RGB-D capture, motion capture ground truth, synthetic data, and 6DoF pose annotations. The project also identifies key limitations, including dataset generalization, tracking jitter, noisy depth data, and the need to monitor LLM hallucinations for inaccurate function executions.
