Context

The Transfer-to-Excellence (TTE) Summer Research Program is a competitive summer internship for California community college students that provides research experience for undergraduates in different disciplines within the College of Engineering. It consisted of 9 weeks of hands-on research experience in the laboratories of UC Berkeley engineering professors. I had the amazing opportunity of being accepted into Professor Seshia’s research group led by his PhD student Edward Kim, along with being part of an amazing program with a great cohort to spend the summer with in the Bay Area, as it was my first time away from my hometown.

Objective

One challenge students face in the classroom of today’s education system is the use of fixed curriculums for any type of subject. Every student who enters a class will come in with a variation of prior knowledge on what is needed for the class, and a variation in their learning curve, as some students are capable of learning the material faster than their peers. Students who need the extra help in a section of the subject would then attend the instructor's office hours, but if the student:faculty ratio is high, this can pose a problem for the instructor to give each student personalized assistance. This is where an Intelligent Tutoring System (ITS) can come in to help, as it can give students a personalized curriculum to each of them depending on their input to the problems it gives them to solve. Using ITS for Virtual Reality (VR), other application domains such as rescue missions and sports could benefit from giving its trainees their own set of training scenarios aimed towards improving the skills they need to work on. Knowledge Tracing is used to track these skills on a knowledge graph and generate a curriculum based on how a trainee performs. For this project, the application domain being used is the Echo Arena VR game.

Overview

This project explores how Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) can be used in Virtual Reality to create personalized training experiences for physical skill development. Rather than relying on a fixed curriculum, the system adapts to each trainee by selecting scenarios based on their demonstrated strengths and weaknesses. The application domain for this project is Echo Arena, a VR esports game that requires players to master a range of movement, throwing, blocking, and strategic skills. To support this adaptive training process, the team first identified 14 core Echo Arena skills through consultation with experienced players. These skills were organized into a knowledge graph that represents prerequisite relationships between abilities. Each node in the graph corresponds to a skill or combination of skills, allowing the system to track what a trainee has learned and what they still need to practice.

After each VR training scenario, the system updates the trainee’s knowledge graph based on whether the scenario was completed successfully. Bayesian Knowledge Tracing is used to estimate the probability that a trainee has mastered a given skill. If a trainee succeeds, the related skill node and its prerequisite nodes can be marked as learned; if they fail, the system can mark that skill and dependent skills as not yet learned. The ITS algorithm then selects the next scenario from the trainee’s Zone of Proximal Development, focusing on skills that are close to being acquired but still need practice.

Scenarios are generated using Scenic, a probabilistic programming language that allows the team to create varied VR training situations efficiently. This makes it possible to generate multiple versions of training scenarios while targeting specific skills in the knowledge graph. The system was implemented in Unity 3D with VR headset testing to ensure that scenarios functioned properly and could be evaluated consistently. Each scenario includes success metrics that determine whether the trainee completed or failed the task. The algorithm can also produce Knowledge Tracing charts that show which skills a trainee has mastered and which areas need further practice. Overall, this project demonstrates the potential of combining Intelligent Tutoring Systems, Bayesian Knowledge Tracing, and VR scenario generation to create adaptive training tools. While the prototype focuses on Echo Arena, the same approach could be applied to other domains that require physical skill training, such as sports, emergency response, or professional simulations.
